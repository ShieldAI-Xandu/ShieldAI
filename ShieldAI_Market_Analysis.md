## 2. The Problem, quantified (your Slides 2–3 ammunition)

**The affordability gap is real and large:**
- A full-time CISO runs **$200K–$400K+/year** — impossible for a business with a handful of employees.
- Yet the foundational security tools and expertise "cost roughly the same whether you have 10 employees or 100" (Cyber Unit, 2026) — so small businesses pay *more* as a % of revenue, making the buy vs. build math even worse for them.

**Most small businesses are dangerously exposed:**
- **47%** of businesses under 50 employees have **zero** cybersecurity budget (StrongDM/CrowdStrike, 2025).
- **51%** have no security measures in place.
- Only **34%** have a formal incident response plan (Guardz, 2025).
- Only **13%** conduct proactive security audits; only **~20%** do annual penetration testing.
- One-third rely on free, consumer-grade tools.

**The cost of doing nothing is quantified — useful for ROI framing:**
- A tested incident-response plan reduces breach cost by **$232,007** (IBM data).
- Small businesses represent **43% of all cyberattacks** (Alpha CIS, 2026).
- SMB attack incident rates climbed **47% year-over-year** (Total Assure, 2025).
- Prevention investment ROI consistently exceeds **7x** across threat categories.

> **Pitch line:** "A minimal security program costing $5K–$15K a year would save
> hundreds of thousands in a breach. The businesses that spend nothing are the
> ones that pay the most."

## 4. Why Now — the demand is being *forced* (your Slide 3)

This is the strongest part of your story. Small businesses aren't choosing to care about security — they're being *compelled*. Three converging pressures:

**A. Cyber insurance is now a gatekeeper.**
- US cyber insurance market: **~$3.2B (2024) → projected $3.9B (2025)**, growing **~22.5% CAGR** to $30B+ by 2035 (Market Research Future).
- **63% of SMBs saw premiums increase 200%+** in 2025 (Total Assure).
- Insurers now demand proof of security controls (MFA, IR plans, etc.) *before* issuing or renewing — "identity-first security has become a non-negotiable requirement to secure any coverage" (SentinelOne, 2026).
- Businesses with **<$25M revenue made 64% of all cyber-insurance claims** in 2025 — insurers are tightening exactly on your target segment.

**B. Big customers push requirements down the supply chain.**
- Larger clients increasingly require vendors/partners to carry cyber insurance and prove controls — "without coverage, you may lose business opportunities or face contract restrictions" (Alpha CIS, 2026).
- Supply-chain attacks carry the highest average claim value (**$318K**) — driving everyone to vet their small vendors.

**C. Regulation is expanding into the small-business tier.**
- HIPAA (healthcare), CMMC (defense contractors), FTC Safeguards Rule, and state privacy laws now carry significant financial penalties.
- CMMC specifically is forcing a wave of small defense-supply-chain manufacturers (like your demo's "Apex Manufacturing") to act.

> **Pitch line:** "We're not betting on small businesses suddenly *caring* about
> security. Their insurers, their biggest customers, and their regulators are
> making the decision for them. Demand is being manufactured by forces outside
> our control — we just have to be there to meet it."

## 5. Market Sizing — TAM / SAM / SOM (build your Slide 9 from this)

> Use a **bottom-up** calculation; investors trust it more than top-down "1% of a
> huge market" hand-waving. Here's a defensible framework — plug in your real
> pricing and target segment.

**TAM (Total Addressable Market):**
- The global SMB cybersecurity spend is **~$109B by 2026**. The vCISO slice is **~$2B+ and growing 15%/yr**. Use the vCISO figure as your honest TAM — it's the category you're in.

**SAM (Serviceable Available Market) — bottom-up example:**
- US small businesses with employees: **~5.8M**
- Narrow to your realistic target: businesses that are regulated, insured, or in a supply chain — e.g. healthcare practices, financial advisors, defense-supply manufacturers, professional services. A conservative slice might be **10–20%** → ~**600K–1.2M businesses**.
- At an example **$3,600/yr** average (blended self-serve + managed), even 600K businesses = a **~$2B+ SAM**.
- *(Replace $3,600 with your real blended ARPU once pricing is set.)*

**SOM (Serviceable Obtainable Market) — what you can realistically capture:**
- Be modest and credible. If you capture **1% of a 600K SAM** at $3,600/yr = **~$21.6M ARR**. State a 3–5 year path to a number like this, not a fantasy.

> **Honesty note:** Don't claim the whole $109B. Investors respect "here's the
> specific, reachable slice and our bottom-up math" far more than a giant top-down
> number. The forced-demand story (§4) is what makes even a modest SOM credible.

## 6. Competitive Landscape (your appendix Competition slide)

The vCISO market is described repeatedly as **"highly fragmented"** — a mix of:

**A. Traditional vCISO / consulting firms** (Fractional CISO, FRSecure, Kroll, BSI, UnderDefense, etc.)
- *Their weakness:* human-only, expensive, doesn't scale, priced for mid-market+ not small business.
- *Your edge:* AI does the heavy lifting, so you serve the small end profitably where they can't.

**B. AI-native vCISO entrants** (Cynomi, RealCISO, Trava, etc.)
- *The real competitors to watch.* Cynomi in particular is an AI-driven vCISO platform for MSPs.
- *Your differentiation must be sharp here:* your deterministic NIST scoring engine (auditor/insurer-trusted), the engineer-in-the-loop quality model, and your specific wedge-to-managed motion.

**C. DIY tools & MSPs** (RapidFire, managed IT providers adding security)
- *Their weakness:* tools without strategy; MSPs lack deep security leadership.
- *Your edge:* you deliver the *CISO function* (strategy, score, program, compliance), not just tooling.

> **Prepare for:** "How are you different from Cynomi?" Have a crisp answer ready —
> likely some combination of: your scoring methodology, your direct-to-SMB +
> managed-services hybrid (not just selling to MSPs), and your roadmap to deployed
> agents + live monitoring. Know their model before pitch day.
